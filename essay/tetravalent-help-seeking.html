Finding 1

Epistemic access predicted who convicted — and who correctly abstained

Validated · n=72

Only agents who could actually triangulate the two hidden fragments landed the real culprit (Dmitri). The single-fragment witnesses — who genuinely cannot know alone — almost never did. That’s not a failure; it’s calibration.

The order isn’t “higher tier, more certainty.” It’s “can you triangulate?” — the relay layer that combines fragments convicts most; the lone witnesses correctly abstain. Abstention is the right answer for the ignorant-who-can’t-triangulate, exactly the founding thesis.

Finding 2

They believed far more than they would say out loud

Robust · both arms

The unexpected catch. Read the agents’ graded belief and they land the culprit ~29% of the time. Ask them to commit out loud and it collapses to ~1.5%. A large, reproducible gap between what is held and what is asserted — present in every arm across 48 runs.

This is why the tetravalent “maybe” has to exist. Force a binary name-or-abstain and you destroy a belief the agent is actually holding. The graded state is where the signal lives — measuring it was the difference between a 100% floor and a readable result.

Finding 3

Method validated

The headline

Do coordinated liars manufacture more false consensus?

Confirmed · n=72

The pre-registered hypothesis. Two liars aiming at the same scapegoat (coordinated) vs each at a different one (independent). At full power the answer is yes — coordinated lying spreads the false name reliably more, and the effect sharpened monotonically as seeds accumulated (p: 0.10 → 0.054 → 0.0004). Small in absolute size, but real: it tilts the collective, it doesn’t dominate it.
